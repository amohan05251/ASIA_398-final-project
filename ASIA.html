Section Two
Rama — Divine God or Human King?
Core Question: How do different cultures reinterpret Rama?
Rama in Madhubani Style
Rama as a child

Overview

The Ramayana spread to Southeast Asia through centuries of trade, migration, and cultural exchange between India and certain South East Asian countries. Along with merchants, Brahmin priests, scholars, and storytellers carried the epic across borders, where it became integrated into local traditions, courts, and performance arts. As a result, the story was not simply retold but adapted to fit each society's religious beliefs, political structures, and artistic styles.

Indian Versions

In the original Indian tradition, especially in the Valmiki Ramayana, Rama is understood as both divine and human at the same time. He is portrayed as a dark, handsome, and strong prince. He is an avatar of Vishnu, yet he moves through the world like a human being: facing exile, separation, and difficult choices.

What stands out most is his commitment to dharma. He is often seen as morally ideal, someone who does the right thing even when it comes at a personal cost. At the same time, he isn’t completely emotionless. There are moments involving Sita, revealing grief, doubt, and inner conflict, which make him feel more human.

There are some other Indian versions where he is portrayed to be more divine and at par with other deities. As a king, Rama represents the model of perfect rule: just, disciplined, and deeply responsible to his people. This is why the idea of rāma-rajya continues to symbolize ideal governance.

Ramakien Mural — Wat Phra Kaew, Bangkok

Thai Version — Ramakien

In the Thai version of the story, the Ramakien, presents Rama as Phra Ram. He has a green complexion, symbolizing his calm power and divine nature. He wears an elaborate golden crown (chada), richly patterned garments, and jewelry. His posture and gestures are graceful and controlled, reflecting composure, nobility, and precision in both leadership and combat. He is both divine and human, but the emphasis often leans more toward his role as an ideal ruler and heroic figure rather than only a god. He is understood as an incarnation of Vishnu (Phra Narai), yet the narrative focuses strongly on how he behaves as a king, a leader, and a person who must make decisions.

Compared to some Indian interpretations, Phra Ram can feel a bit more emotionally present, especially through his relationships with his family, particularly Nang Sida (Sita) and his brother Phra Lak. At the same time, he still represents strong moral values such as discipline, righteousness, and loyalty. Kingship is a major theme in the Thai tradition. Phra Ram is portrayed as the model of a just and compassionate monarch, and his character has even influenced how Thai kings are symbolically connected to the idea of righteous rule. In fact, Thai monarchs go by the name Rama (insert number). For example, it was King Rama I who compiled all the versions of the Ramakien and organised the latest version of it, after the original parts were lost.

Reamker Gallery — Angkor Wat, Cambodia, 12th century

Cambodian Version — Reamker

In the Cambodian Reamker, Rama, known as Preah Ream, is understood as having a divine origin, but the story places much stronger emphasis on his human qualities and moral character. He is portrayed with a green complexion as well, emphasizing serenity. He has soft, symmetrical facial features and almond-shaped eyes that convey wisdom and compassion. He wears a golden mokot crown, layered silk garments, and intricate jewelry, and his youthful, athletic physique emphasizes his qualities as a noble warrior-prince. While both the Thai and Cambodian versions present him as an ideal ruler,

the Thai version focuses more on his royal image and composure, whereas the Cambodian version highlights his inner struggles, relationships, and moral choices. The Reamker allows more room to reflect on his decisions. As a king, Preah Ream represents compassion and ethical responsibility, making him feel less like a distant perfect ruler and more like a human leader trying to do what’s right. Both the Cambodian and Thai Ramayanas are nuanced by Buddhist principles, which heavily focus on karma and the idea that good always prevails over the bad.

Wayang Puppet of Rama — Javanese Tradition

Indonesian Version

In the Indonesian tradition, especially in texts like the Ramayana Kakawin and later versions such as the Hikayat Seri Rama, Rama is portrayed more as a human and exemplary hero than a fully divine figure. He is shown as a heroic and noble figure, often with a human-looking appearance rather than glowing divinity. He wears Javanese-style royal attire, sometimes with symbolic or magical elements like enchanted weapons, emphasizing his virtue, intelligence, and moral strength rather than supernatural perfection. His divinity is often downplayed, especially in Islamic-influenced retellings, where he becomes someone to admire rather than worship. In fact, he is integrated into an Islamicate sense of history where he is held to be a descendant of the Prophet Adam, not a God. Compared to the Thai and Cambodian versions, these stories place greater emphasis on emotional depth and character interactions, and even allow moments where Rama can be questioned. For example, the character Trijata, who supports Sita, does not hesitate to criticize Rama when he doubts her loyalty, which is something rarely seen in other traditions. This makes Rama feel less distant and more human. Kingship is still central, but it is expressed through moral guidance and wisdom, with Rama often explaining what makes a good ruler rather than simply representing perfection.

Feminist Reinterpretations of Sita
Is Sita a symbol of strength or oppression — or both?

Introduction

The character of Sita in the Ramayana has long been a subject of debate. According to the traditional view, she is regarded as the ideal wife and embodiment of virtue representing devotion, purity, and sacrifice. However, modern interpretations challenge this singular view, asking whether she is merely a symbol of oppression or a figure of inner strength and resilience.

By comparing traditional portrayals with contemporary reinterpretations, it becomes clear that Sita can be understood as both a symbol of strength and oppression, depending on the perspective through which her story is told.

Traditional Portrayals of Sita

In traditional interpretations of the Ramayana, Sita is established as the ultimate example of marital loyalty and moral integrity, while simultaneously serving as a profound icon of internal fortitude (Sharma, 2023).

She is traditionally viewed as the perfect example of a devoted wife (pativrata), shown through her devotion to Rama, even during times of hardship and struggle. She is considered “ideal” because of her willingness to sacrifice, especially when she chose to leave the comfort of the palace to follow Rama into a dangerous fourteen-year exile, believing it was her duty to share his life.

Her identity is also closely connected to her purity, which she proves through the trial by fire (Agni Pariksha) after being held captive by Ravana. Throughout the story, she is portrayed as a graceful and dignified daughter, queen, and wife.

Although some interpretations see her as submissive, Sita is believed to be very strong and resilient. Her strength is not loud or aggressive, but comes from her inner determination and emotional strength. This is especially clear during her time in Lanka, where she stays calm and refuses Ravana’s advances, even when threatened. Her courage is so powerful that even Ravana eventually recognizes it.

This portrayal perhaps emphasizes the qualities that belong to what was considered an ideal wife during that time period as qualities such as obedience, loyalty, and self-sacrifice. However, this idealization also reflects the gender expectations of the time, where a woman’s virtue was closely tied to her chastity and obedience to her husband. As a result, while Sita is revered, she is also confined within a framework that limits her agency.

Additionally, Sita’s portrayal in traditional dance and theatre is largely shaped by devotional storytelling, where emphasis is placed on her purity, grace, and devotion rather than her inner conflict or agency. In performance traditions such as Ramlila, Sita is typically shown as gentle, composed, and emotionally expressive. Her movements are often soft and controlled, symbolizing modesty, while her dialogue (when present) highlights loyalty and love towards Rama and acceptance of her duties as a wife.

In classical Indian dance forms like Bharatanatyam and Kathak, Sita is usually represented through expressive gestures called mudras and facial expressions called abhinaya, which emphasize devotion, separation from Rama, and moments of suffering or longing. These performances tend to focus on key emotional episodes, such as her exile, abduction by Ravana, and eventual reunion, rather than her lack of agency. Sita is often portrayed as a figure who endures hardship with grace rather than actively shaping the course of events. Overall, traditional theatre and dance present Sita as an idealized moral figure which aligns with traditional views.

Modern Portrayals of Sita

Modern retellings move away from showing Sita as only an obedient and ideal wife, presenting her instead as a more independent and emotionally complex character. In The Forest of Enchantments, Chitra Banerjee Divakaruni retells the Ramayana from Sita's point of view which allows readers to understand her thoughts, feelings, and struggles more deeply. In this version, Sita is not silent; she questions the expectations placed on her and demonstrates strength in how she responds to difficult situations.

Other modern works, such as Sita's Sister by Kavita Kane and The Liberation of Sita by Volga, explore her character by focusing on relationships, choices, and personal growth encouraging readers to see her not merely as a symbol of devotion, but as a strong and thoughtful individual who actively shapes her own story.

The Agni Pariksha can be seen as both a sign of her inner strength and an example of patriarchal oppression making it one of the most contested moments in the entire epic.

Comparison of the Two Views

Traditional views frame Sita's power through inner resilience and silent endurance as the ideal pativrata, whereas modern reinterpretations emphasize her active agency and emotional complexity as an independent individual. A central point of comparison is the Agni Pariksha, which traditionalists interpret as a triumph of moral integrity but modern critics view as a symbol of patriarchal oppression and unfair gender standards.

Ultimately, Sita cannot be defined solely as a symbol of either strength or oppression , she embodies both. Her endurance, resilience, and moral integrity demonstrate immense strength, while the societal expectations placed upon her reveal elements of oppression. The interpretation of her evolves with time, reflecting the values and concerns of each era, ensuring that her character remains relevant and critically engaged across generations.

Section Four
Modern Media Influence
How the Ramayana evolved across modern visual culture
Ramanand Sagar's Ramayan — Doordarshan, 1987

Ramayan (1980s)

The 20th-century series closely follows the narrative structure of the Valmiki Ramayana and Tulsidas's Ramcharitmanas. Director Ramanand Sagar reportedly read fourteen versions of the Ramayana before settling on this reference. His stated mission was to bring to audiences the virtuous story of Maryada Purushottam Shri Ram — Rama as the ideal, boundary-respecting man.

The choice of Valmiki's text was a deliberate act of cultural and religious caution. The story was already deeply familiar to North Indian audiences through devotional songs and Ramlila performances. Rama shown as a clear incarnation of God gave the series the quality of a devotional experience helping reduce criticism. The show communicated values such as obedience to parents, loyalty in marriage, devotion to duty, and respect for elders through an ideal role model people could aspire to imitate.

Recentering the Narrative — Sita's Perspective

Siya Ke Ram (2000s)

Unlike the earlier series, Siya Ke Ram does not retell the story in a devotional or linear manner. It reinterprets the epic by shifting narrative focus toward Sita (Siya) offering a perspective that had not been central in traditional retellings. Though the show draws from classical texts, it does not strictly follow any single source, allowing creators to explore emotional depth and Sita's inner world in unprecedented ways.

Both Rama and Sita are presented as emotionally complex and more human. Events such as exile, sacrifice, and duty are portrayed with ethical complexity, prompting audiences to think critically rather than simply accept. This reflects a broader 21st-century shift toward diverse, underrepresented perspectives treating the epic as a living, evolving narrative rather than a fixed devotional text.

Ramayana: The Legend of Prince Rama — 1992 Animated Film

Expansion Across Contemporary Visual Media

The evolution of the Ramayana extends beyond television into film, animation, and graphic literature. Ramayana: The Legend of Prince Rama presents the narrative in an accessible, action-driven format for younger global audiences. By contrast, Ramayan 3392 A.D. situates the narrative within a futuristic dystopian setting suggesting the Ramayana functions as a flexible narrative framework, not only a religious text. Modern cinematic adaptations like Adipurush attempt to merge traditional storytelling with contemporary visual effects, though mixed reception reveals that reinterpretation remains bounded by cultural and religious sensitivities.

Tension Between Tradition and Innovation

Across these forms of media, characters are increasingly portrayed as emotionally complex. Figures such as Sita gain greater narrative agency, and antagonists like Ravana are occasionally reinterpreted with psychological depth. Morality evolves from absolute and prescriptive to interpretive and open to discussion for example, reflecting broader societal changes in views on gender, morality, and individual agency, while the Ramayana's sacred status continues to set limits on how far transformation can go without controversy.

A Living Tradition

The Ramayana has endured not because it is unchanging, but because it is adaptable. Across more than two millennia, it has been rewritten, reperformed, and reinterpreted to meet the needs of each era and culture that receives it from Valmiki's Sanskrit epic to Thai court murals, from Bengali devotional poetry to modern television.

Art Reinterprets, Not Just Preserves

Each artistic form such as miniature painting, classical dance, television and animation has made deliberate choices about which values to foreground. Art has never passively carried the Ramayana's morality; it has actively shaped it, making the story mean something different for each audience.

A Consistent Core

Despite vast differences in theology and cultural context, one thing remains constant across all versions: the insistence that righteous conduct, loyalty, and the struggle between good and evil are worth telling stories about. The theological framing shifts; the moral architecture does not.

Universal Structure, Local Meaning

The Ramayana became the Thai Ramakien, the Cambodian Reamker, and the Malay Hikayat Seri Rama precisely because each culture claimed it as their own. Its structure is universal enough to travel but its meaning is always locally negotiated. It is both a culturally specific tradition and a universal moral story, depending on who is telling it and why.
